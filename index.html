Analysis of Coordinated Disinformation Activity by IRA Twitter Accounts (2012–2018)

“How many voices does it take to make a lie sound like the truth?”

“Can we catch a propaganda campaign in the moment it is being born?”

“When does a message stop being an opinion and become a coordinated narrative?”

The Core Question

“How did the IRA's coordinated Twitter activity differ in structure, timing, and narrative across political communities (Right vs Left) during the 2016 U.S. election period?”

Between 2014 and 2018, the Internet Research Agency (IRA) carried out one of the most extensive foreign influence campaigns ever exposed on a major social platform. Through thousands of fabricated personas (activists, citizens, journalists, entertainment accounts) the IRA infiltrated conversations, manipulated sentiment, and amplified divisive narratives throughout key moments of the U.S. political cycle.

This project reconstructs that operation using the public dataset of IRA-linked Twitter accounts. By analyzing millions of interactions, we explore how the network functioned, which tactics it used, and why certain accounts became disproportionately influential.

Our aim is not simply to describe the activity, but to understand the mechanism behind it: its timing, its internal hierarchy, and its narrative strategies.

DATASET OVERVIEW

To understand the scale of the operation, we analyzed a massive dataset of tweets attributed to the Internet Research Agency (IRA). This data reveals not just the volume of content, but the meticulous categorization of accounts designed to infiltrate specific American communities.

The dataset spans six years, but the intensity of activity peaks dramatically during the 2016 election cycle. By dissecting the metadata, we can see the "digital fingerprints" of the operatives: account creation dates, posting devices, and the specific dialects mimics employed.

Persona Analysis

The IRA operated through a wide range of personas, but the region and language breakdowns reveal a clear focus:

  • The United States is the dominant reported region.
  • English is the most used language by a large margin.

Since the 2016 U.S. elections are the central context, this justifies restricting the analysis to English-language tweets linked to U.S.-focused activity. Within this subset, we concentrate on Left Troll and Right Troll accounts—not because they are the largest categories overall, but because they directly map onto the partisan structure of U.S. political discourse and are most relevant to election interference.

Conclusion: The Anatomy of a Spike

Strategic Rationale The strategy shifted from aiding one candidate to wounding the entire country. The trolls exploited the real-world crisis in Charlottesville to amplify racial and political conflict ("Charlottesville," "Antifa," "violence") and reinforce tribal political identity ("maga," "Trump"). The massive spike in activity was intended to drive the maximum possible division post-election. The peak coincided with the US government's official statements regarding Russian involvement in the election hacks and the WikiLeaks release of stolen DNC/Podesta emails. The trolls' messaging had two aims: 1. Flood the environment with apolitical content ("music," "listen," "album") to distract left-leaning voters and drown out coverage of the hacking's origins. 2. Inflame social tensions ("black," "police") to discourage voter participation by fostering distrust and disaffection with the political process.
