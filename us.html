INSIDE CALIFORNIA’S ROAD NETWORK

Uncovering the Patterns Hidden in California’s Road System

“What is a road network, beyond the map we see?”

“Why do real road systems tend to be planar?”

“Why do small recurring motifs reveal so much?”

The Core Question

What local recurring structures (motifs), such as triangles, exist in California’s road network?

California’s varied landscape shapes how its roads can be built. In SNAP’s roadNet-CA dataset, this entire system becomes a graph of almost two million intersections connected by more than five million road segments.

Seen this way, the network stops being a map and becomes a structure shaped by constraints: long linear chains, few alternative paths and small repeating patterns. These motifs reveal where the system is resilient, where it is fragile and how a single missing link can disconnect entire areas.

DATASET OVERVIEW

The roadNet-CA dataset from SNAP represents the entire road network of California. Each intersection is treated as a node and each road segment as an undirected edge.

The dataset includes:

  • 1,965,206 nodes (intersections)
  • 2,766,607 edges (road segments)

Because the whole state forms one large connected structure, the dataset is ideal for studying sparsity, local redundancy and motifs in real-world infrastructure.

Source:

What this snapshot shows

California’s road network behaves as one giant, continuous system.

Most intersections connect to only two others, creating long linear chains and leaving very little local redundancy. Triangles—tiny pockets of structural backup—are rare and mostly confined to dense urban regions. The result is a network that is globally connected but locally sparse, a structure shaped as much by geography as by design.

Inside the Structure of California’s Roads

Below, we explore three key structural patterns that define California’s road network, from global connectivity to local motifs.

Degree Distribution & Connectivity

Most intersections connect to only two others. This creates long, linear chains that stretch across the state and reveal a network built under physical constraints rather than free growth.

Takeaway: California’s road system is structurally sparse, with few highly connected intersections.

Triangle Motifs: Local Redundancy

Triangles are rare in road networks because physical constraints prevent many alternative paths. Where they do appear, they signal redundancy — a small pocket of resilience.

Takeaway: Triangle motifs highlight the only places where the network offers true backup routes.

A Close-Up of a Critical Intersection

Zooming into a high-degree node shows short chains radiating outward, few local cycles, and a quick drop in density after two hops.

Takeaway: Even major intersections sit within neighborhoods that offer very limited alternative routing.
